# Glossary of Key Terms: NDPA and GDPR

**Reference:** Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023 (NDPA) | EU General Data Protection Regulation 2016/679 (GDPR)
**Prepared by:** TrustGrid Technology

---

## How to Use This Glossary

This glossary defines key terms as they are used under the NDPA and GDPR. Where the two frameworks use different terminology for the same concept, both terms are included. Where the definitions differ between the two frameworks, the differences are highlighted.

---

## A

**Adequacy Decision**
A formal determination that a country, territory, or sector provides a level of data protection equivalent to the framework in question.
- Under GDPR: Issued by the European Commission. Countries with adequacy decisions include Japan, Canada, and the UK (post-Brexit).
- Under NDPA: The NDPC maintains its own whitelist of countries deemed adequate. The two lists are different and must be checked separately.

**Administrative Fine**
A financial penalty imposed by a regulatory authority for violation of data protection obligations.
- Under GDPR: Up to €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover for the most serious violations.
- Under NDPA: Up to ₦10 million or 2% of annual gross revenue, whichever is greater.

**Anonymisation**
The process of irreversibly altering personal data so that the individual cannot be identified directly or indirectly. Truly anonymised data falls outside the scope of both the NDPA and GDPR.

**Automated Decision Making**
A decision made by automated means without any meaningful human involvement.
- Under GDPR: Governed by Article 22. Individuals have the right not to be subject to solely automated decisions that produce legal or similarly significant effects.
- Under NDPA: Governed by Section 37. Similar protections apply with exceptions for consent, contract, and statutory authorisation.

---

## B

**Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)**
Internal rules adopted by a multinational group of companies to allow transfers of personal data within the group to countries outside the EU that do not have adequacy decisions.
- Under GDPR: Recognised as a valid transfer mechanism. Must be approved by the lead EU supervisory authority.
- Under NDPA: Not explicitly addressed. BCRs approved under GDPR do not automatically satisfy NDPA transfer requirements.

**Breach Notification**
The obligation to notify a regulatory authority and in some cases affected individuals when a personal data breach occurs.
- Under both frameworks: Regulator must be notified within 72 hours of becoming aware of a breach.

---

## C

**Compliance Audit Return (CAR)**
An annual compliance audit report that must be filed with the NDPC by Data Controllers and Processors of Major Importance under the NDPA.
- Filing deadline: 31 March each year.
- No equivalent exists under GDPR.

**Consent**
A freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous indication of a data subject's agreement to the processing of their personal data.
- Under both frameworks: Consent must be as easy to withdraw as it is to give. Pre-ticked boxes and bundled consent do not constitute valid consent.

**Controller**
See Data Controller.

**Cross-Border Data Transfer**
The transfer of personal data from one country to another.
- Under both frameworks: Transfers to countries without adequate data protection are restricted and require specific safeguards.

---

## D

**Data Controller**
An entity that determines the purposes and means of processing personal data.
- Under GDPR: Referred to as a "controller."
- Under NDPA: Referred to as a "data controller." The NDPA also uses the term "Data Controller of Major Importance" (DCMI) for entities that meet specific thresholds.

**Data Controller of Major Importance (DCMI)**
A classification under the NDPA for data controllers that process personal data of more than 200 data subjects within 6 months, operate in sectors of major economic importance, or handle data in a fiduciary capacity.
- DCMIs face enhanced compliance obligations including mandatory DPO appointment, annual CAR filing, and engagement of a licensed DPCO.
- No direct GDPR equivalent although GDPR imposes heightened obligations on certain types of processing regardless of entity classification.

**Data Minimisation**
The principle that personal data collected and processed should be adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary for the specified purpose.
- Recognised under both frameworks.

**Data Processing Agreement (DPA)**
A contract between a data controller and a data processor governing how the processor handles personal data on behalf of the controller.
- Required under both frameworks before a processor can handle personal data on a controller's behalf.

**Data Processor**
An entity that processes personal data on behalf of a data controller.
- Under GDPR: Referred to as a "processor."
- Under NDPA: Referred to as a "data processor."

**Data Protection Authority (DPA)**
The regulatory body responsible for enforcing data protection law.
- Under GDPR: Each EU member state has its own DPA. For cross-border cases, the lead DPA is typically in the country where the controller has its main establishment.
- Under NDPA: The Nigeria Data Protection Commission (NDPC) is the single national DPA.

**Data Protection Compliance Organisation (DPCO)**
An organisation licensed by the NDPC to provide data protection compliance services including conducting compliance audits under the NDPA.
- Unique to the NDPA. There is no equivalent licensing requirement under GDPR.
- Organisations subject to the NDPA must engage a licensed DPCO for their annual CAR filing.

**Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)**
A process to identify and minimise data protection risks associated with a new project or processing activity.
- Required under both frameworks when processing is likely to result in high risk to individuals.
- Mandatory triggers include large scale processing of special category data, systematic monitoring of publicly accessible areas, and use of new technologies.

**Data Protection Officer (DPO)**
A designated individual responsible for overseeing an organisation's data protection compliance program.
- Under GDPR: Required for public authorities, organisations conducting large scale systematic monitoring, and organisations processing large scale special category data.
- Under NDPA: Required for DCMIs processing data of more than 200 data subjects within 6 months and organisations in sectors of major economic importance.
- Under NDPA: DPO must be registered with the NDPC.

**Data Subject**
An identified or identifiable natural person whose personal data is being processed.
- Definition is consistent across both frameworks.

**Data Subject Access Request (DSAR)**
A request from a data subject to access their personal data held by an organisation.
- Under GDPR: Must be responded to within one calendar month.
- Under NDPA: Must be responded to without undue delay. Apply the GDPR 30-day standard to satisfy both frameworks.

---

## E

**Erasure**
The deletion or destruction of personal data. Also known as the right to be forgotten.
- Recognised under both frameworks. Individuals can request erasure when data is no longer necessary, consent is withdrawn, or processing is unlawful.

**European Data Protection Board (EDPB)**
The independent EU body that ensures consistent application of GDPR across EU member states and coordinates enforcement between national DPAs.
- No equivalent exists under the NDPA.

---

## G

**General Application and Implementation Directive (GAID)**
Operational guidance issued by the NDPC that supplements the NDPA. The GAID took effect on 19 September 2025 and replaced the NDPR 2019 as the primary subsidiary instrument under the NDPA.
- No GDPR equivalent. GDPR guidance is issued by the EDPB and individual DPAs.

**GDPR**
The EU General Data Protection Regulation 2016/679. The primary data protection law of the European Union, applicable since 25 May 2018.

---

## L

**Lawful Basis**
The legal justification for processing personal data. Both frameworks require that every processing activity has a valid lawful basis.
- Both frameworks recognise six lawful bases: consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, and legitimate interests.

**Legitimate Interests**
A lawful basis for processing where the controller has a genuine and proportionate interest in processing personal data that is not overridden by the rights and interests of the data subject.
- Requires a balancing test under both frameworks. The balancing test must be documented.

---

## N

**NDPA**
The Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023. The primary data protection law of Nigeria, signed into law on 12 June 2023.

**NDPC**
The Nigeria Data Protection Commission. The independent regulatory authority established by the NDPA to enforce data protection law in Nigeria.

**NDPR**
The Nigeria Data Protection Regulation 2019. The predecessor to the NDPA. Substantially superseded by the NDPA 2023 and replaced by the GAID from 19 September 2025.

---

## P

**Personal Data**
Any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person.
- Both frameworks define personal data broadly to include direct identifiers such as names and ID numbers as well as indirect identifiers such as IP addresses, device IDs, location data, and online identifiers.

**Privacy by Design**
The principle that data protection should be considered and built into systems and processes from the outset rather than added as an afterthought.
- Recognised under both frameworks.

**Processing**
Any operation performed on personal data including collection, storage, use, disclosure, transfer, and deletion.
- Definition is consistent across both frameworks.

**Profiling**
Automated processing of personal data to evaluate, analyse, or predict aspects of an individual such as their behaviour, preferences, creditworthiness, or location.
- Regulated under both frameworks. Individuals have rights in relation to profiling that produces significant effects.

**Pseudonymisation**
The processing of personal data in such a way that it can no longer be attributed to a specific data subject without the use of additional information kept separately.
- Recognised under both frameworks as a security measure that reduces risk but does not remove data from the scope of either law.

---

## R

**Record of Processing Activities (ROPA)**
A documented record of all personal data processing activities carried out by an organisation.
- Required under both frameworks for organisations above certain thresholds.
- Under GDPR: Required for organisations with 250 or more employees or whose processing carries specific risks.
- Under NDPA: Required as part of the accountability obligations for all data controllers and processors.

**Right to Data Portability**
The right of a data subject to receive their personal data in a structured, commonly used, and machine-readable format and to transmit it to another controller.
- Recognised under both frameworks where processing is based on consent or contract and carried out by automated means.

---

## S

**Special Category Data**
Personal data that is particularly sensitive and requires additional protection.
- Under both frameworks: Includes health data, biometric data, genetic data, racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, religious beliefs, and criminal records.
- Under GDPR: Also explicitly includes sexual orientation.
- Processing of special category data requires an explicit lawful basis in addition to a general lawful basis.

**Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)**
Standard contractual provisions approved by a regulatory authority that can be used as a safeguard for cross-border data transfers.
- Under GDPR: Issued by the European Commission. Updated SCCs were published in 2021.
- Under NDPA: Recognised as a transfer mechanism. The NDPC is developing specific requirements for SCCs under the NDPA framework.

---

## T

**Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA)**
An assessment conducted before transferring personal data to a third country to evaluate whether the legal and practical protections in the destination country are sufficient to uphold the rights of data subjects.
- Required under both frameworks when relying on SCCs for cross-border transfers.

---

## V

**Vital Interests**
A lawful basis for processing personal data where processing is necessary to protect the life of a data subject or another person.
- Recognised under both frameworks. Typically applies in emergency situations involving risk to life.
