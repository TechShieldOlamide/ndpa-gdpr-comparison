# Key Differences Between the NDPA and GDPR

**Reference:** Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023 (NDPA) | EU General Data Protection Regulation 2016/679 (GDPR)
**Prepared by:** TrustGrid Technology

---

## Overview

The NDPA and GDPR share the same philosophical foundation: personal data belongs to individuals and organisations that process it must do so responsibly, transparently, and with accountability.

But the two frameworks differ in important ways. Understanding those differences is what separates a compliance program that actually works from one that only looks good on paper.

This document covers the most practically significant differences between the two frameworks.

---

## 1. Mandatory Annual Audit Filing

**NDPA:** Data controllers and processors of major importance (DCPMIs) must file a Compliance Audit Return (CAR) with the NDPC every year by 31 March. This is a formal, mandatory filing with Nigeria's regulator.

**GDPR:** No equivalent mandatory annual audit filing exists. GDPR operates on an accountability principle, meaning organisations must maintain records and be able to demonstrate compliance when asked, but there is no annual filing requirement with a regulator.

**Why it matters:** Nigerian businesses often assume that maintaining good internal records is sufficient. Under the NDPA, you must also file externally with the NDPC on a fixed annual deadline. Missing this deadline is a compliance failure in itself regardless of how good your internal program is.

---

## 2. Licensed Compliance Organisations (DPCOs)

**NDPA:** Organisations subject to the NDPA must engage a Data Protection Compliance Organisation (DPCO) that is licensed by the NDPC to conduct their compliance audits. You cannot self-audit for the purposes of your CAR filing.

**GDPR:** No equivalent requirement exists. Organisations can conduct internal audits, engage external auditors of their choice, or use any qualified privacy professional. There is no licensing requirement for the auditor.

**Why it matters:** This is one of the most significant structural differences between the two frameworks. Under the NDPA, the choice of who audits you is constrained by the NDPC's licensed list. Under GDPR, you have full flexibility. Nigerian businesses operating under both frameworks must ensure their DPCO engagement satisfies NDPA requirements while their broader compliance program satisfies GDPR accountability obligations.

---

## 3. Penalty Structure and Scale

**NDPA:** Maximum fine of ₦10 million or 2% of annual gross revenue, whichever is greater. Single tier penalty structure.

**GDPR:** Two tier structure. Less serious violations attract fines of up to €10 million or 2% of global annual turnover. Most serious violations attract fines of up to €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover.

**Why it matters:** In absolute terms GDPR fines are significantly larger. The €1.2 billion fine against Meta and the €746 million fine against Amazon demonstrate the scale of GDPR enforcement for large organisations. However the NDPC has shown it is willing to act: the ₦766.2 million fine against Multichoice Nigeria and the $220 million action against Meta Platforms in Nigeria signal serious enforcement intent. Do not underestimate either regulator based on the penalty ceiling alone.

---

## 4. Data Protection Officer Thresholds

**NDPA:** A DPO is required for organisations that process personal data of more than 200 data subjects within a 6 month period, operate in sectors of major economic importance, or handle data in a fiduciary capacity such as financial services.

**GDPR:** A DPO is required when processing is carried out by a public authority, when core activities involve large scale systematic monitoring of individuals, or when core activities involve large scale processing of special category data.

**Why it matters:** The NDPA threshold is considerably lower and more broadly applicable. A Nigerian fintech processing data of 200 or more users within 6 months, which describes almost every active Nigerian digital business, needs a DPO under the NDPA. The GDPR threshold is higher and more specifically defined. Many small businesses that need a DPO under NDPA would not need one under GDPR alone.

---

## 5. Cross-Border Transfer Mechanisms

**NDPA:** The NDPC maintains its own whitelist of countries deemed to have adequate data protection. Transfers outside this whitelist require specific safeguards. The NDPA also recognises Standard Contractual Clauses as a transfer mechanism but the specific requirements are still being developed by the NDPC.

**GDPR:** The European Commission issues adequacy decisions for specific countries. Transfers outside adequacy decisions require SCCs, Binding Corporate Rules, or other approved mechanisms. The SCCs are detailed and prescriptive documents issued by the European Commission.

**Why it matters:** The two whitelists are different. A country that has received an adequacy decision from the European Commission is not automatically on the NDPC whitelist and vice versa. Businesses transferring data in both directions need to assess their transfer mechanisms against both frameworks separately. BCRs recognised under GDPR do not automatically satisfy NDPA transfer requirements.

---

## 6. Response Timeframes for Data Subject Requests

**NDPA:** Requires response without undue delay but does not specify an exact number of days in the same prescriptive way as GDPR.

**GDPR:** Requires response within one calendar month of receiving the request. This can be extended by a further two months for complex or numerous requests provided the individual is informed within the first month.

**Why it matters:** The GDPR standard of one month is more prescriptive and is the benchmark most compliance programs are built around. For businesses operating under both frameworks, applying the GDPR one month standard to all data subject requests is the safest approach as it satisfies the stricter of the two requirements and provides a clear operational target.

---

## 7. Children's Data

**NDPA:** Provides protections for children's data and requires parental consent for processing data of minors but the specific age thresholds and implementation details are less prescriptive than GDPR.

**GDPR:** Sets the age of digital consent at 16 years old by default with member states able to lower this to a minimum of 13. Requires verifiable parental consent for children below the applicable age threshold. Specific provisions around children's data in online services are well developed.

**Why it matters:** Digital products and services targeting or likely to be accessed by children need to carefully assess their obligations under both frameworks. The GDPR standard is more developed and detailed. Applying GDPR-level protections for children's data satisfies both frameworks.

---

## 8. Automated Decision Making

**NDPA:** Section 37 grants individuals the right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing when those decisions have legal or significant consequences. Exceptions apply for consent, contract necessity, or statutory authorisation.

**GDPR:** Article 22 provides a similar right with similar exceptions. GDPR guidance on automated decision making and profiling is more developed through European Data Protection Board guidelines and enforcement decisions.

**Why it matters:** For Nigerian fintechs and AI companies using automated systems for credit scoring, fraud detection, customer profiling, or hiring decisions, both frameworks impose obligations around transparency, human oversight, and the right to contest automated decisions. The NDPC has signalled it is reviewing the NDPA specifically to address AI and automated decision making more directly. Businesses using AI in high-stakes decisions should build human review mechanisms now under both frameworks.
